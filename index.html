NEPQ "Brush-off" Cheatsheet

Never "handle" an objection. Ask questions to get behind the concern and help the prospect overcome it themselves. [cite: 879-880]

"I'm just looking for pricing." or "Can you just send me a quote?"
Core NEPQ Principle: Never give a proposal or quote without first diagnosing the problem. A "Trusted Authority" must diagnose before they prescribe.
  • "That's not a problem. Just so I can put together the *best* information for you, what exactly are you looking for?"
  • "I'd be open to putting together a proposal for you, but I'm not quite sure I could even help you yet... ...could I ask a few questions about your situation... to be able to put something together for you that might be useful?
    *(Then ask your first Situation Question):* 'For example, what are you using now... [for your fleet]?'"
  • "I can get you that. What is it that you are hoping to see from the quote?"
  • "I will get to work on putting together a quote for you... Now, let's assume... we get you the quote and we were able to meet your needs... What do you see as the next step?" [cite: 749-751]

"I'm too busy right now." or "Can you call me back later?"
Core NEPQ Principle: A "Trusted Authority's" time is also valuable. Do not be "needy". Establish professional posture and set a *firm* next step. [cite: 771-773]
  • "That's not a problem. I can give you my number and you'll have to call me back if you'd like?"
  • "My number is [Your Number]. What's your timeframe on getting back to me just to see if I would be available for you?"
  • "If you have your calendar handy, I could pull out mine to book a specific time with you just so you don't have to chase me down and vice versa... would that be appropriate?"

"This isn't a priority for me right now." or "I'm not interested."
Core NEPQ Principle: This simply means they do not feel enough *pain* or urgency. Your job is to find out if there is *any* problem to solve. [cite: 671-672]
  • "I understand. I was just curious, what prompted you to look into changing this... [by requesting info] in the first place?"
  • "That's fair. It sounds like things are going fairly well for you... is there anything you would change about... [how you manage your fleet] if you could?"
  • "I see. How important is it for you to solve this problem... [of not knowing where your vehicles are]? Why is that [not a priority] now though?" [cite: 184, 340-341]
  • "I understand. Have you given up trying to deal with this problem?"

"We're already using [Competitor]." or "We have a vendor for that."
Core NEPQ Principle: This is a "Vendor Disruption" opportunity. Do *not* pitch your features. Your only goal is to find the *gap*—the small dissatisfaction they have. [cite: 437-438]
  • "That's great. To me, it sounds like things are going fairly well for you with [Competitor]... is there anything you would change about... [their service] if you could?" *(If they answer):* "Why change that though?"
  • "If you could change one thing about who you use now, what would that be?"
  • "Can I ask in what ways could your vendor do better for you than what they are doing now though?"
  • "Back when you chose to work with that company... what was your selection criteria...? In what ways has that (criteria) changed as you look at your needs today though?" [cite: 288-289]

"Can you send me some references?"
Core NEPQ Principle: This is often a way to get rid of you. You must qualify their intent and secure a next step. [cite: 711]
  • "That's not a problem, I'm curious what would you like to ask them when you call?" (This uncovers their real concern.) [cite: 716]
  • "So that I can send the right people to you, what specifically would you like to discuss with them?" [cite: 719]
  • "I can reach out to them... Now, let's pretend for a moment that the clients you talk to say good things... where do you think we should go from there?" (This is the key qualifying/commitment question.) [cite: 726]

Your NEPQ Mindset: Problem Finder, Not Product Pusher
Your goal is to help them find a problem they didn't know they had, or one they didn't know they could solve.
  • You are a "Trusted Authority" and a "Problem Finder," like a doctor.
  • You are NOT a "product pusher."
  • You are here to ask questions and *diagnose*, not to pitch, present, or persuade.
  • People buy on **emotion** (pain) and justify with logic. Your job is to find the *emotional* reason for change.
  • DON'T talk about your products (GPS, dash cams). DO ask questions about their problems (fuel costs, driver behavior, lost time).
  • Your TONE is more important than your words. Be neutral, calm, and curious (like a doctor), not "salesy" or "enthusiastic."
  • Use a skeptical or confused tone when asking "why" questions to pull out more emotion.
  • Without pain, there is no sale.

How to Dig Deeper (Probing & Clarifying Questions)
Use these when you get short, logical, or vague answers. These questions find the *emotion* and *pain*.
  • "Tell me more..." [cite: 379]
  • "How do you mean exactly?" [cite: 317]
  • "Can you be more specific? Give me an example..." [cite: 323]
  • "What's causing this issue?" [cite: 318]
  • "Why so important now?" [cite: 322]
  • "Has that had an impact on you? In what way?" [cite: 147]
  • "How did you feel about that?" [cite: 391]
  • "What would this do for you personally?" [cite: 329]

How to Uncover the Decision Process
Use these *before* the transition to understand *how* they buy, so you don't get stuck later.
  • "Can you walk me through the criteria you use when picking a company to solve these issues...?" [cite: 366]
  • "Let's suppose you are looking at several different companies who all meet your criteria, including price. How would you then decide what to do?" [cite: 369]
  • "In addition to you, who else recognizes that this is a major problem in the company?" [cite: 472]
  • "Who else besides you would be involved in approving the budget to solve this?" [cite: 517]

"We don't have the budget for this." or "That's too expensive."
This means you haven't built enough value by finding their *pain*. The problem you've found isn't big enough (yet).
  • "And I can appreciate that money might be an issue... from what you told me, how do you think you can resolve that so that you can..." [Repeat back the solution they said they wanted] [cite: 524-525]
  • "You had mentioned that the most important thing to you is cost, can I ask how that compares to you actually getting results and being able to solve this problem though?" [cite: 371-372]
  • "Tell me, if you *did* have the funding... is this something that would work for you? ...Why do you feel it would though?" [cite: 522-523]
  • "If you don't decide to address this problem now, how much will it cost you this year in lost... [profit/fuel/etc]?" [cite: 489]

How to "Present" (by NOT Presenting)
After the "Transition Question," your "presentation" is just proving you can solve their stated problems. Ask these "Checking for Agreement" questions *constantly*. [cite: 633, 649]
  • "Does that make sense?" [cite: 641]
  • "Are we on the same page?" [cite: 642]
  • "What are your thoughts on that?" [cite: 643]
  • "Are you with me on this?" [cite: 644]
  • "Do you see how that works?" [cite: 645]
  • "Do you see how that could help you?" [cite: 646]
  • "How do you see that helping you the most?" [cite: 651]

What to do AFTER you pivot (The One-Call Close Framework)
This is the start of your One-Call Close. You have successfully pivoted and are now a "Problem Finder." Accelerate this entire NEPQ Dialogue Framework into one conversation.
  • Situation Questions You: "...What are you using now for your fleet?"
    Prospect: "I just use my phone and paper logs."
  • Problem Awareness Questions You: "And what do you like or... what *don't* you like about that?"
  • Consequence Questions You: "What if you don't do anything about this problem and your fuel costs keep going up?" [cite: 172-173]
  • Qualifying Questions You: "How important is it for you to solve this problem? Why is that important to you *now* though?" [cite: 184]
  • Transition Questions You: "Based on what you told me... [repeat their problems/feelings]... Does that feel as if it might be what you're looking for?" [cite: 196-197]
  • Committing Questions You: "Do you feel like this could be the answer for you? Why though? ...Really, the next step is we make some type of arrangement..." [cite: 206-208]
